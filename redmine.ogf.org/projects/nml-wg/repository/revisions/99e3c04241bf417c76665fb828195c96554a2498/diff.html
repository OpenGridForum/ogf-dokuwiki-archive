\subsubsection{Topology and Node}


  		





  
  127
  
    

  		





  
  128
  
    
A Topology and Node behave similar: they both contain inbound ports and outbound ports, and can contain a SwitchingService to allow creation of internal links (cross connects) from inbound ports to outbound ports. Especially with the abiity to create logical, sliced or virtual devices, the distinction is getting blurred.


  		





  
  129
  
    

  		





  
  130
  
    
The distinction is that a Node is located at a single geographic location, while a Topolgy is a set of geographically disperse Network Elements.

\subsubsection{Hierarchical Topology}


  		





  
  133
  
    

  		





  
  134
  
    
Larger networks may want to publish details of their network topology as a whole, as well as detaild about regional segments, without publishing details of the actual devices. NML allows the publication of a hierarchical Topology tree, where the top-level Topology has a \emph{hasTopology} relation with smaller Topologies. These smaller Topologies must be fully enclosed -- the \emph{hasTopology} relation can not be used to relate partial overlapping Topologies.


  		





  
  135
  
    

  		





  
  136
  
    
For example, a \emph{Topology} \textsc{A} may want to publish about two parts of its Topology, \textsc{A\_West}, and \textsc{A\_East}. This allows it to publish difference in connectivity and costs between the two parts. It can do so with the following relations:


  		





  
  137
  
    

  		





  
  138
  
    
    \nmlrelation{Topology A}{\vrule width0pt height1.5ex}{hasTopology}{}{Topology A\_West}\\


  		





  
  139
  
    
    \nmlrelation{Topology A}{\vrule width0pt height1.5ex}{hasTopology}{}{Topology A\_East}
